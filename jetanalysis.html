Jet Content Analysis in Hadronic Collisions

During my sophomore summer vacation and the duration of my senior year, I worked on the research project that would become my senior thesis.

Initially, work was started at Wayne State University through their REU program, where I was assigned the task of processing large quantities of data in order to perform statistical analysis and determine whether certain observables the team had previously posited were physically significant.

The project flow was relatively simple, with a three step process used to create data, analyze and break that data down, then perform the necessary analysis and print graphs.


1. Data generation

For data generation, we used a Monte Carlo-based event generator known as Pythia, which is capable of creating fairly accurate particle collision events given a large number of specified initial conditions. For this project, we simulated the 2013 ATLAS LHC p-p runs so we had a verified collection of datato perform sanity checks on our own results. This data was then saved to a custom structure breaking individual resultant particles into their base information, which was stored into another event structure. These were then saved off to a file for further processing by the next phase.


2. Data processing (jet finding)

In the second stage, we take the output information from Pythia and process the resultant particles to search for the jets we want to find. In this case, a jet is a high-energy particle which is ejected from the point of impact at high transverse velocity. Using FastJet, we are able to take the particle data from Pythia and get a set of grouped vectors of particles which belong to what FastJet believes is a jet. These are then saved off to another file, similar to the process in stage one.


3. Data analysis and graphing

In the third and final stage, the collected data and information from stage two is taken and general average statistics are generated from the multiplicity of events. This gives us the averages we require to calculate the observables which have been developed by the team long before I arrived. Using ROOT, we print these observables and helpful statistics as histograms, generally as functions of multiplicity.
